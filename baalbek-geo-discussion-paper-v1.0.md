### ABSTRACT

The three massive limestone monoliths in the quarry adjacent to the Baalbek temple complex, Hajar al-Hibla (the Stone of the Pregnant Woman, c.1,000 tonnes), the Stone of the South (c.1,242 tonnes), and the Forgotten Stone discovered in 2014 (c.1,650 tonnes), have been studied principally in the context of Roman construction logistics. The conventional interpretation holds that they were quarried for the Temple of Jupiter but never transported due to weight or structural defects.  

This paper examines whether an alternative interpretation merits consideration: that these stones, or at least the primary stone Hajar al-Hibla, may have served a fixed function at their quarry location, possibly related to geodetic, astronomical, or reference-system purposes predating the Roman period. The paper presents measurable data, notes where patterns are intriguing, acknowledges where they are inconclusive, and proposes a programme of empirical tests that could move the question toward a more evidenced resolution.  

No supernatural, extraterrestrial, or speculative mechanism is invoked. The observations presented here operate within the framework of ancient human capability and long-term cultural continuity at a site with documented occupation extending at least 11,000 years.

### SECTION 1: CONTEXT AND THE CENTRAL QUESTION  

#### 1.1 The Site  

Baalbek, known as Heliopolis under Greek and Roman influence, occupies an elevated position in Lebanon's Beqaa Valley at approximately 1,150 metres above sea level. Archaeological evidence documents continuous human habitation at the site from at least 9,000 BCE. The Roman temple complex, including the Temple of Jupiter with its famous Trilithon foundation stones, represents one of the most ambitious construction programmes in the ancient world.  

Approximately 900 metres from the main temple complex lies an ancient limestone quarry containing three monoliths of extraordinary scale, the largest worked stones known to have been quarried in human history. None of these three stones was ever incorporated into any known structure.  

#### 1.2 The Question That Has Not Been Formally Answered  

This paper explores a related but distinct possibility: that the stone's primary function was not structural or symbolic in the Roman sense, but instrumental, serving as a fixed physical reference point. This is not presented as a conclusion. It is presented as a question that the available evidence suggests is worth asking rigorously.  

#### 1.3 What This Paper Is Not  

This paper does not argue that the stones were placed by a lost advanced civilisation, extraterrestrial intelligence, or any agency beyond known human capability. It does not dismiss the mainstream archaeological interpretation. It acknowledges that the conventional explanation, Roman construction material abandoned due to weight limitations or structural defects, is reasonable and supported by evidence. It asks whether that explanation fully accounts for all observed characteristics of the stones and their relationships to other ancient sites.  

#### 1.4 Categories and Limitations

**Categories**:  

- Verified measurements: distances, bearings, and orientations calculated from known coordinates  
- Observations: patterns identified within those measurements  
- Hypotheses: interpretations that require further testing  

**Limitations**:

- No independent direct dating of the quarry stones has been performed  
- Orientation measurements are approximate and not yet confirmed to sub-degree precision  
- Statistical significance of global alignments has not yet been tested against random baselines  
- Astronomical correlations require specialist modelling and confirmation  

Maintaining this distinction is essential to avoid conflating measured data with interpretation.

### SECTION 2: PHYSICAL CHARACTERISTICS AND OBSERVED ANOMALIES  

#### 2.1 The Three Quarry Stones  

| Stone | Mass (est.) | Long-axis orientation | Notable characteristics |  
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Hajar al-Hibla (Stone of the Pregnant Woman) | ~1,000t | ~43° magnetic / 38.5° True North | Partially attached to bedrock. Structural crack on NW face (2015 study). |
| Stone of the South | ~1,242t | Consistent with quarry grid | Rediscovered 1990s. Shares orientation pattern. |
| The Forgotten Stone (Third Monolith) | ~1,650t | Nearly identical to above | Discovered 2014. Positioned beneath Hajar al-Hibla. |  


#### 2.2 The Orientation Observation  

The Temple of Jupiter at Baalbek has a documented surveyed azimuth of approximately 75°30'. The long axis of Hajar al-Hibla is oriented at approximately 43° magnetic, or 38.5° True North after correction for local magnetic declination of +4.5° East. This represents a separation of approximately 32-37° between the stone's axis and the temple's axis.  

Although the orientation of the native stone in the quarry has to be considered, if Hajar al-Hibla were pre-cut construction material intended for placement in the temple, one might expect its orientation to approximate the temple axis to reduce the logistical burden of rotation during transport. Rotating a monolith of this mass by 32° during transport would represent an extraordinary additional challenge with no parallel in documented Roman construction practice. This does not prove the stone was not construction material, but it raises a question about the orientation that the conventional interpretation does not fully address.  

#### 2.3 The Redundancy Observation  

The structural crack documented on the northwestern face of Hajar al-Hibla in 2015 has been cited as the reason for its abandonment. The Forgotten Stone, 65% heavier, positioned beneath the cracked stone, sharing an identical quarry orientation grid, was discovered only in 2014.

**One observation worth considering**: If the crack explains why Hajar al-Hibla was abandoned, it does not immediately explain why a second, larger stone sharing the same orientation was also never removed. The co-existence of two stones of this scale, the same orientation, and the same non-removed status at the same location invites a question. Is it possible the larger stone represents a planned replacement for the cracked original, rather than a second independent abandoned construction attempt? This interpretation, which might be termed the redundancy hypothesis, would account for both stones simultaneously and parsimoniously, though it requires physical investigation to evaluate.  

#### 2.4 Pre-Roman Evidence at the Site  

Several independent lines of evidence are relevant to the question of whether the megalithic elements at Baalbek predate the Roman period:  

- The Trilithon foundation stones exhibit differential weathering compared to the Roman temple structures above them, a pattern noted by multiple researchers as potentially indicating significantly greater age.  
- No Roman literary source documents the construction, commissioning, or engineering of the Grand Terrace, unusual for what would have been Rome's most ambitious construction project.  
- The German Archaeological Institute's geophysical surveys have revealed pre-Roman occupational layers beneath the Roman structures, with pottery and artefacts indicating earlier construction phases.  
- Graham Hancock has observed that the quarry stones were never cut down for incorporation into the Roman temple, a practical step that would have been routine if Romans had quarried them and subsequently found them unusable at full size.  
- Archaeological evidence places human presence at Baalbek from at least 9,000 BCE, a timeframe consistent with the astronomical epoch discussed in Section 3 of this paper.

These observations do not establish a pre-Roman date for the quarry stones. They suggest the question of date remains genuinely open and worth pursuing through direct investigation.

### SECTION 3: ASTRONOMICAL CONSIDERATIONS  

#### 3.1 The Temple Orientation - An Established Archaeoastronomical Finding.  

Peer-reviewed research by Giulio Magli (2016) established that the Temple of Jupiter at Baalbek is oriented to approximately 75°30', corresponding to the heliacal rising of the Pleiades during the Herodian era. This finding is well-supported and provides a baseline for comparison with the stone's different orientation.  

#### 3.2 The Stone's NE Axis - A Possible Earlier Reference  

The stone's NE axis at 38.5° True North points in a different direction from the temple and corresponds to a different astronomical reference. Precession models suggest this bearing may correspond to the rising azimuth of Vega (Alpha Lyrae) at the latitude of Baalbek during approximately the 10th millennium BCE.  

Submitted archaeoastronomical data for Baalbek's latitude (33.99°N) in 10,450 BCE, cross-verified against independent spherical trigonometry, indicate:  

| Star | Dec. 10,450 BCE | Rising azimuth | Relationship to stone axis (38.5°) |
| :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- |
| Vega (alpha Lyrae) | +43°30' | 37.8° NE | 0.7° — closest match identified (provisional; subject to verification) |
| Capella | +54°40' | 14.8° NNE | ~24° offset — not a match |
| Deneb | ~+58–62° | Circumpolar | Never rises at this latitude — eliminated |  


The correspondence between the stone's axis and the calculated rising azimuth of Vega is sensitive to modelling assumptions and should be treated as provisional pending specialist verification.  

These figures require confirmation through specialist astronomical software (Stellarium, using NASA/JPL DE430/431 ephemeris data) accounting for proper motion, atmospheric refraction, and local horizon elevation. They are presented here as indicative rather than confirmed.  

#### 3.3 The Significance of Vega at This Epoch  

If the Vega alignment were confirmed, a contextual observation adds potential significance. Vega was the North Pole Star at approximately 12,000 BCE. By 10,450 BCE it was transitioning from its pole position and beginning to appear on the northeastern horizon, a phenomenon that would have been among the most dramatic observable astronomical events of that era. Whether this transition was regarded as astronomically significant by people living at Baalbek in this period cannot be determined from available evidence, but the context is worth noting.  

#### 3.4 A Pattern of Long-Term Continuity  

If the stone's axis reflects an astronomical reference from approximately 10,450 BCE, and the temple's axis reflects an updated astronomical reference from approximately the 1st century BCE, the same sacred location at Baalbek would have been used to encode astronomical references across a span of approximately 10,000 years. This would represent an extraordinary example of long-term site continuity, but would not be without precedent. Göbekli Tepe in Turkey shows evidence of astronomical encoding across multiple construction phases, and peer-reviewed research (Magli, 2015) has established that its enclosures tracked stellar reappearances in the 10th millennium BCE.  

#### 3.5 The Apex Angle - A Geometric Observation  

The triangle formed by Giza, Baalbek, and Stonehenge produces an apex angle at Stonehenge of 10.465°, calculated from independently verified distances. Separately, research on the Orion Correlation (Bauval, 1994) places the minimum precessional altitude of Orion's Belt above the Giza horizon at approximately 10.45° in 10,450 BCE. The near-coincidence of these two values, 10.465° and 10.45°, is noted here as a geometric observation rather than a claim of intentional encoding. Whether this represents meaningful convergence or numerical coincidence is a question that quantitative analysis of comparable random triangles could address.

#### 4.2 The Base Module Observation  

The great-circle distance between the Great Pyramid at Giza (29.9792°N, 31.1342°E) and Hajar al-Hibla at Baalbek (34.0042°N, 36.2171°E) is 655.624 km. This value is noted as a potential base unit (M) in the context of the relationships described below. Whether it functions as a designed unit of measurement or simply as a reference distance is not claimed, it is used here as a computational anchor.

The alignment of both Stonehenge and Teotihuacan within 1.5° of the same geometrically derived bearing from Baalbek, two sites on different continents, separated by over 8,700 km from each other, is an observation that invites quantitative assessment. It could be coincidence. It could reflect something more systematic. Without a rigorous statistical test against random baselines, neither conclusion is justified.  

#### 4.4 The Americas Isosceles Triangle  

Giza to Teotihuacan: 12,323.760 km.  
Baalbek to Teotihuacan: 12,381.374 km.  
Difference: 57.6 km across approximately 12,300 km, a variance of 0.468%.  
Giza and Baalbek are 655 km apart.  
Teotihuacan is equidistant from both within 57.6 km.  

This near-isosceles relationship across half the Earth's circumference is quantitatively precise and warrants formal statistical testing. Without comparison against appropriate random baselines, its significance cannot yet be determined. For this to occur by chance would require Teotihuacan to have been built at accidentally equal distance from two specific Old World reference points. The probability of this occurring randomly has not been formally calculated in this paper, doing so rigorously, with appropriate controls, is one of the recommended investigations.  

#### 4.5 The Easter Island Circumference Relationship  

Baalbek to Easter Island is 16,646.915 km.  
This distance corresponds to a 150° arc of a great circle, representing a precise 5/12 division of the Earth’s 360° circumference relative to the Baalbek datum.  
Five-twelfths of Earth's mean circumference (40,075 km) is 16,697.917 km. The difference is 51 km, an accuracy of 99.69%.  

Easter Island is the most isolated inhabited location on Earth. Whether this relationship reflects intentional placement or coincidence is not claimed. It is noted as a measurement worthy of formal statistical evaluation.

### SECTION 5: THE NAME HIBLA - A COMPARATIVE LINGUISTIC OBSERVATION  

#### 5.1 Beyond Folk Legend  

The conventional explanations for the name Hajar al-Hibla draw on local folk traditions. A pregnant woman who claimed to know how to move the stone, or pregnant jinn assigned the task of quarrying. These are culturally authentic and deserve respect as oral tradition.  

A comparative linguistic observation worth noting. Terms evoking pregnancy, the seed, or the navel as a sacred origin point appear independently across multiple ancient cultures. Whether this represents independent convergence on a shared metaphor, cultural diffusion, or the preservation of a technical vocabulary is not claimed. However, the pattern is noted.  

| Culture | Term | Literal meaning | Associated concept |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Ancient Levant | Hibla | Seed / pregnancy | Origin point
| Ancient Greek | Omphalos | Navel | Centre of the world
| Easter Island (Rapa Nui) | Te Pito Kura | Navel of the World | Sacred centre stone |
| Sanskrit | Nabhi | Navel / centre | Cosmic axis point |
| Ancient Egyptian | Benben | Primordial mound | Origin of creation |
| Gobekli Tepe (orig.) | Portasar | Navel Mountain | Sacred hill / reference point |  


Whether the name Hajar al-Hibla preserves a memory of the stone's geodetic significance, encodes a symbolic statement about power, or reflects entirely local legend cannot be determined from available evidence. The cross-cultural convergence of the navel-origin metaphor at sites associated with sacred geography is an observation that comparative linguists and archaeologists may find worth examining independently.

### SECTION 6: THE INSTRUMENTATION QUESTION  

#### 6.1 An Observation About Precision  

The precision observed in the construction of the Giza plateau, orientation to within 0.05° of True North across a 230-metre base, a level surface accurate to 2.1 cm, requires a real-time feedback mechanism for measurement, checking, and correction. No instrument capable of producing these tolerances has been recovered from Giza, Baalbek, Stonehenge, or any other site in the network discussed here.  

This is not evidence that such instruments did not exist. It is evidence that if they existed, they did not survive or have not yet been identified. The question of what measurement and calibration system was used to achieve the documented tolerances at these sites has not been answered in the archaeological literature. It is one of the most consequential open questions in the study of ancient engineering.  

#### 6.2 The Precision Requirement  

A calculation relevant to any geodetic interpretation. A 0.1° angular error at Baalbek produces approximately a 29 km miss at the distance of Easter Island (16,647 km). Any system that placed Easter Island at 5/12 of Earth's circumference from Baalbek to within 51 km was operating to angular tolerances on the order of 0.17° or better. Whether ancient surveyors could achieve this precision is a question that existing evidence at Giza, where orientation tolerances of 0.05° are documented, suggests should not be dismissed without investigation.

### SECTION 7: MAINSTREAM INTERPRETATIONS AND HOW THEY RELATE TO THIS ANALYSIS  

**The mainstream interpretation**: All three quarry stones were quarried by Romans for the Temple of Jupiter and abandoned due to transport difficulty or structural defects, is supported by:

- The proximity of the stones to the temple  
- The similarity of quarrying technique to other Roman sites, and  
- The general Roman practice of using oversized quarry blocks that were cut down to size on site.

This paper does not dispute these observations. It asks whether they fully account for:  

- The 32° orientation offset from the temple  
- The consistent orientation grid shared by all three stones  
- The presence of a second stone beneath the cracked stone sharing the same grid, and  
- The relationships to distant sites that this analysis has identified.  

These questions are raised not as contradictions of the mainstream interpretation, but as observations it does not yet address.

| :--- | :--- | :--- |

### CONCLUSION  

The Stone of the Pregnant Woman at Baalbek, Hajar al-Hibla, is the largest worked stone known to have been quarried in human history. It remains in its quarry, partially connected to bedrock, with a structural crack on one face and a significantly larger companion stone sharing the same orientation grid positioned beneath it.  

The conventional explanation holds that it was quarried for the Roman temple and abandoned due to its weight and the crack. This is a reasonable explanation. This paper asks whether it is a complete one.  

The observations presented here, the orientation offset from the temple, the shared grid of all three stones, the mathematical relationships to distant ancient sites, the etymological parallels across six independent cultures, the convergence of three independent lines of evidence on the epoch of 10,450 BCE, do not individually or collectively constitute proof of an alternative interpretation. They constitute, as suggested here, a sufficient body of intriguing measurement to justify a more systematic investigation than has yet been undertaken.  

The most important single investigation remains unconfirmed. To perform forensic analysis of the bedrock connection surfaces to determine whether the stone was being separated or was never intended to be. That analysis could, in principle, resolve the central question of this paper. It is respectfully suggested that this should be done.  

This paper does not resolve the question of the Baalbek quarry stones' original intention. It presents reproducible measurements, documented observations, and open questions in the hope that they may contribute to a more fully evidenced answer. All data is independently verifiable. All calculations are reproducible.

Critical review and independent verification are welcome and encouraged.
